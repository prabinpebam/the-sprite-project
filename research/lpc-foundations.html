Context

LPC Foundations

The Liberated Pixel Cup - the openly licensed art body and style guide that nearly every project in this research is built on.

TL;DR

LPC began as an OpenGameArt.org art competition to produce a large, style-consistent, openly licensed sprite set. That corpus - 64x64 modular characters with a shared style guide - became the substrate for generators, engine tooling, and countless add-on art packs. Understanding its vocabulary and licenses is prerequisite to understanding every other source here.

Origin

The concept of the Liberated Pixel Cup was introduced by Bart Kelsey and Chris Webber. It was originally a competition on OpenGameArt.org, sponsored by Creative Commons, Mozilla, and the Free Software Foundation, with the goal of creating a body of artwork sharing a common style. (Those organizations sponsored the original contest only; they are not involved in the present-day tools.)

How the paradigm evolved

  1. LPC - the competition & base set

    A large body of compatible 64x64 art. Original animations: spellcast, thrust, walk, shoot, slash, and hurt, for male and female adult bases.

  2. makrohn's Universal spritesheet

    An XCF combining the PNG assets - effectively the first (offline) LPC spritesheet generator, later the seed for the web generator.

  3. Universal LPC (ULPC)

    Expanded bases and animation sizes; ensured assets covered all bases/animations. Its most notable addition was weapons with oversize animation frames.

  4. LPC Revised (LPCR)

    Changes proposed by ElizaWy: in places a changed number/order of animation frames, a new color palette, and smaller heads.

  5. LPC Expanded (LPCE)

    Further animations (bow, climb, run, jump) and new bases (child, elderly). Many assets are not yet drawn for these - an open "help wanted" frontier.

The vocabulary

LPC
The original competition, and now the body of work and the style that "LPC" art follows.
ULPC (Universal LPC)
LPC expanded with new animation sizes/bases; the basis of the flagship generator. Added oversize weapon frames.
LPCR (LPC Revised)
ElizaWy's revision: reworked frames, new palette, smaller heads.
LPCE (LPC Expanded)
Additional animations (bow, climb, run, jump) and bases (child, elderly).
Base frame
64x64 pixels; weapons and some effects use oversize frames that exceed the base grid.

The license landscape

LPC art is not a single license. Each asset is distributed under one or more open licenses, and the most permissive available option determines how freely a given piece can be used. This is the single most important practical constraint on building anything with LPC.

Attribution is mandatory (except CC0)

If you use generated sprites or images from an LPC spritesheets directory, you must credit every contributing author. Generators help by exporting a CREDITS.csv/text file for exactly the layers you selected. For DRM platforms (Steam, iOS App Store), sticking to CC0 and/or OGA-BY assets is the safe choice.

Related artwork beyond characters

Although the flagship repository focuses on character sprites, LPC also includes many tilesets and other artwork; tileset collections live on OpenGameArt.org. Two of the art sources in this research - Pirates and Horses - are examples of the community continually extending that corpus.
